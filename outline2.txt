Section 5: It Generalizes — RNNs and Language (5 min | 8 slides)


Slide 21 — Meet Yoshua Bengio


- Most cited computer scientist globally

- Most cited living scientist across all fields

- Neural probabilistic language model — word embeddings

- "Third author. You know what's coming."

Slide 22 — The Language Problem


- Old approach: N-grams, counting symbol frequencies

- The curse of dimensionality: \(V^N\) possible sequences

- Brittle, slow, no understanding of meaning

Slide 23 — The Exponential Advantages of Deep Networks


- Two reasons deep networks are powerful for language: 1. \(n\) binary features
	→ \(2^n\) possible combinations

	2. Each layer multiplies expressive power exponentially


- "30 neurons can represent over a billion concepts"

Slide 24 — Word Embeddings


- One-hot encoding diagram

- First layer creates activation patterns per word

- Foreshadows Word2Vec

- The Tuesday/Wednesday, Sweden/Norway example

- "They didn't program it to learn this. It just did."

Slide 25 — Simple Feed Forward Language Model


- Given 5 words, predict the next

- Works surprisingly well on both small clean datasets and large messy ones

- Frame: "This was contrary to what people expected"

Slide 26 — RNNs


- The problem with feed forward for sequences

- RNN diagram, unrolled

- Can translate English to French by learning the semantic rules of both
	languages

- The vanishing gradient problem — transition to LSTM

Slide 27 — LSTMs


- Invented 1997

- Diagram of the cell state and gates

- Intuition: memory with a forget switch

- The authors are bullish — and they're right

Slide 28 — The Memory Bottleneck


- The biggest remaining problem: neural networks are stateless

- Memory is inherently stateful

- Neural Turing Machines (DeepMind, 2014)

- Differentiable Turing machine with attention-based read/write

- "They've identified the exact problem. The solution is two years away."

Section 7: Then Transformers Happened (3 min | 5 slides)


Slide 34 — 2017: A New Architecture


- Brief recap of what transformers are — your class already covered this

- Don't re-teach it, reference it: "You've seen this before. Now you know what
	problem it was solving."

- Attention is All You Need discards recurrence entirely

Slide 35 — What Transformers Got Right That the Paper Missed


- The paper assumed RNNs were the right backbone for sequence modeling

- Transformers showed you don't need recurrence at all if attention is powerful
	enough

- The memory bottleneck they identified was real — but the solution wasn't
	Neural Turing Machines, it was self-attention

Slide 36 — What the Paper Got Right About Transformers (Without Knowing It)


- Unsupervised pretraining: GPT is exactly this

- Attention as the key mechanism: they named it

- Scale matters: they said it, transformers proved it

- "They predicted the destination without predicting the vehicle"

Slide 37 — The Conclusions That Still Hold


- Deep learning over hand-engineered features: obviously true

- Scale + data + compute: validated completely

- Representation learning as the foundation: yes

- What's still open: reasoning, grounding, true language understanding

Slide 38 — Where Are We Now


- Brief, honest: LLMs are extraordinary but not AGI

- The paper's philosophical open question still stands: "New paradigms are
	needed to replace rule-based manipulation of symbolic expressions"

- We have better tools, the question isn't answered
